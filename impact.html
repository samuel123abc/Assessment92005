The Impact of Native Species Decline

New Zealand is home to some of the most unique plants and animals in the world. But many of these species are disappearing. This isn't just a problem for wildlife, it affects our forests, our culture and our future.

"He taonga tuku iho - Our native species are treasures handed down to us. Their loss is everyone's loss."

1. Damage to Ecosystems

New Zealand’s native species play a crucial role in maintaining the health of our ecosystems, many of which have evolved in isolation over millions of years. Birds like the kererū are one of the only species capable of swallowing and dispersing the large seeds of native trees such as tawa and karaka, helping forests regenerate naturally. Insects like wētā and native beetles help decompose plant material and recycle nutrients back into the soil. When these species decline due to predators like rats, stoats, and possums or from habitat loss, these natural systems begin to collapse. Without seed dispersal, native trees struggle to grow. Without decomposers, soil quality worsens. Invasive plants and pests can take over, creating a fragile and unbalanced ecosystem. This not only harms biodiversity but also affects things people depend on, like clean water, fertile land for farming, and the natural carbon storage forests provide to help fight climate change.


2. Cultural Impact

For Māori, native species are more than just part of the environment — they are taonga (treasures) with deep cultural, spiritual, and historical significance. Many species are linked to whakapapa (genealogy), stories, and identity. Birds like the kākāpō and tūī appear in traditional waiata (songs), pūrākau (stories), and carvings. Trees like the kauri and pōhutukawa are sacred and feature in creation stories and important ceremonies. The decline or extinction of these species is not just a scientific concern — it represents the loss of mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge), ancestral connections, and intergenerational identity. When these species disappear, so too does part of the cultural heritage of Aotearoa. Protecting native species is therefore a vital part of honouring Te Tiriti o Waitangi and preserving the unique relationship Māori have with the natural world.
