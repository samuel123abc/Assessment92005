1. Introduced Predators

New Zealand’s native species evolved in isolation for millions of years, without land mammals. The arrival of humans — first Māori, then Europeans — brought predators such as rats, stoats, ferrets, and possums. These animals prey on eggs, chicks, and adult birds, especially species like the kiwi, tīeke (saddleback), and mohua.

Many birds are flightless or nest on the ground, making them extremely vulnerable. Without natural defenses, even a small population of predators can wipe out entire colonies.

2. Habitat Loss

Before human arrival, over 80% of New Zealand was covered in forest. Today, much of this has been cleared for farming, cities, and infrastructure. Wetlands — once abundant — have been reduced by over 90%.

When forests and wetlands disappear, so do the food sources, nesting areas, and climate conditions that native species rely on. Fragmentation also isolates populations, reducing their chance to breed and survive.

3. Climate Change

Global warming has serious consequences for native species. Alpine habitats are shrinking as temperatures rise. Changes in rainfall affect food availability and breeding cycles. Warmer seas impact marine species like seabirds and fish.

Species with narrow ranges — like the rock wren or native geckos — are especially at risk because they can’t simply move uphill forever.

4. Pollution

Pollutants from farms, cities, and industry damage waterways and soil. Fertilizers and pesticides contaminate habitats and can poison insects, fish, and birds. Microplastics have been found in marine wildlife.

Native freshwater species such as eels (tuna) and whitebait are sensitive to changes in water quality and flow.

5. Human Activity

Off-road vehicles, hikers, construction, and tourism can all disturb nesting birds, trample rare plants, or spread pests. Roads often fragment habitat and increase the risk of native species being hit by cars (e.g. penguins, wētā).

Even well-meaning people can unintentionally spread weed seeds or pathogens into vulnerable areas.

6. Invasive Plants and Insects

Non-native plants can crowd out native bush, altering the structure of ecosystems. Insects like wasps compete with birds for nectar or prey on native larvae.

These threats reduce biodiversity and weaken the balance of native food webs.

7. What Can Be Done?

Education, conservation, and policy change are all essential. You can help by:

  • Joining Predator Free NZ groups
  • Volunteering in native planting and trapping projects
  • Reducing waste and supporting eco-friendly practices
  • Donating to conservation organisations like Forest & Bird or DOC
